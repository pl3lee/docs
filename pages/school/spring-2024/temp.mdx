### Integration (Continued)
#### Important Example
Here is an important example. Consider $\int_C \frac{1}{z}$, where $C$ is the unit circle, traversed in the positive direction. We note that $\frac{1}{z}$ does not have an antiderivative which applies to all of $C$.

**Method 1**:

Split $C$ up. Let's start at $-i$.

![](/images/school/spring-2024/pmath332/17-1.png)

So we have
$$
\int_C \frac{1}{z} dz = \int_{C_1} \frac{1}{z} dz + \int_{C_2} \frac{1}{z} dz
$$
On $C_1$ we may use $\text{Log}(z)$.
$$
\begin{align*}
  \int_{C_1} \frac{1}{z} dz &= \text{Log}(z) \Big|_{-i}^i \\
  &= \text{Log}(i) - \text{Log}(-i) \\
  &= \frac{\pi}{2}i - \left(- \frac{\pi}{2}i\right) = \pi i
\end{align*}
$$
On $C_2$, we may use $\log_0(z)$.
$$
\begin{align*}
  \int_{C_2} \frac{1}{z} dz &= \log_0(z) \Big|_i^{-i} \\
  &= \log_0(-i) - \log_0(i) \\
  &= \frac{3\pi}{2} i - \frac{\pi}{2}i = \pi i
\end{align*}
$$
Thus $\int_C \frac{1}{z} dz = 2\pi i$.

**Method 2**
Parameterize $C$. Use $z = e^{it}$, $t \in [0, 2\pi]$. Differentiate to get $dz = ie^{it} dt$. So
$$
\begin{align*}
  \int_C \frac{1}{z} &= \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{e^{it}} ie^{it} dt \\
  &= \int_0^{2\pi} i dt = 2\pi i
\end{align*}
$$
What if we change the radius? We have $z = Re^{it}$, $dz = Rie^{it} dt$, so
$$
\begin{align*}
  \int_C \frac{1}{z} &= \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{Re^{it}} Rie^{it} dt \\
  &= \int_0^{2\pi} i dt = 2\pi i
\end{align*}
$$
