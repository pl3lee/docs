# Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors
Suppose that we have a solution to the following problem:
$$
\begin{align*}
    Av = \lambda v 
\end{align*}
$$
where $A$ is a square matrix, $v$ is a vector, and $\lambda$ is a scalar. Then, $v$ is called an eigenvector of $A$ and $\lambda$ is called an eigenvalue of $A$.

## How to Find Eigenvalues
To find the eigenvalues of a matrix, we need to solve the following equation:
$$
\begin{align*}
    \det(A - \lambda I) = 0
\end{align*}
$$
As an example, suppose we have the following matrix:
$$
\begin{align*}
    A = \begin{bmatrix}
        2 & 1 \\
        1 & 2
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{align*}
$$
We solve the following to find its eigenvalues:
$$
\begin{align*}
    \det(A - \lambda I) &= 0 \\
    \det\begin{bmatrix}
        2 - \lambda & 1 \\
        1 & 2 - \lambda
    \end{bmatrix} &= 0 \\
    (2 - \lambda)(2 - \lambda) - 1 &= 0 \\
    4 - 4\lambda + \lambda^2 - 1 &= 0 \\
    \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 &= 0 \\
    (\lambda - 3)(\lambda - 1) &= 0 \\
    \lambda &= 3, 1
\end{align*}
$$
So, the eigenvalues of $A$ are $\lambda = 3, 1$.

## How to Find Eigenvectors
We find eigenvectors associated with each eigenvalue by solving the following equation:
$$
\begin{align*}
    (A - \lambda I)v = 0
\end{align*}
$$
Using the example from above, we first find the eigenvector associated with $\lambda = 3$:
$$
\begin{align*}
    (A - 3I)v &= 0 \\
    \begin{bmatrix}
        2 - 3 & 1 \\
        1 & 2 - 3
    \end{bmatrix}v &= 0 \\
    \begin{bmatrix}
        -1 & 1 \\
        1 & -1
    \end{bmatrix}v &= 0 \\
    \begin{bmatrix}
        -1 & 1 \\
        0 & 0
    \end{bmatrix}v &= 0 \\
    -v_1 + v_2 &= 0 \\
    v_1 &= v_2
\end{align*}
$$
So an eigenvector is $\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$. Next, we find the eigenvector associated with $\lambda = 1$:
$$
\begin{align*}
    (A - I)v &= 0 \\
    \begin{bmatrix}
        2 - 1 & 1 \\
        1 & 2 - 1
    \end{bmatrix}v &= 0 \\
    \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 1 \\
        1 & 1
    \end{bmatrix}v &= 0 \\
    \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 1 \\
        0 & 0
    \end{bmatrix}v &= 0 \\
    v_1 + v_2 &= 0 \\
    v_1 &= -v_2
\end{align*}
$$
So an eigenvector is $\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}$. Indeed, we can check that
$$
\begin{align*}
    A\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix}
        2 & 1 \\
        1 & 2
    \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = 3\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \\
    A\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix}
        2 & 1 \\
        1 & 2
    \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = 1\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}
\end{align*}
$$
